Pierre Dantas, Lucas Cordeiro, Waldir Junior • arXiv:2605.26169 • 25 May 2026 (v2: 30 May 2026)

This survey traces 17 years of ESBMC development from a single-threaded ANSI-C prototype to a platform supporting nine front-ends (C, C++, CUDA, CHERI, Kotlin, Python, Rust, Solidity, and more), documenting 43 competition awards at SV-COMP and Test-COMP and industrial deployments including a defence engagement at Lockheed Martin and the VeriBee spin-off. The paper records the recent coupling of ESBMC with Large Language Models and autonomous AI agents for self-healing software and loop-invariant generation, and outlines future directions including deeper LLM integration and expanded safety-standard compliance. Economic impact is quantified at over GBP 9.3 million and EUR 4.98 million in confirmed public research funding.

Bounded Model Checking

ConVer: Using Contracts and Loop Invariant Synthesis for Scalable Formal Software Verification

Muhammad A. Pirzada, Weiqi Wang, Yiannis Charalambous, Konstantin Korovin, Lucas C. Cordeiro • arXiv:2605.27051 • 26 May 2026

ConVer addresses state-space explosion in Bounded Model Checking of large C programs by decomposing verification top-down: an LLM synthesises function contracts from the target system property, then a CEGAR-CEGIS loop alternates system-level and function-level checks, refining contracts via SMART ICE learning whenever a check fails. Evaluation on 45 Frama-C benchmark programs shows 82–96% verification success across three LLM backends, with 93–95% of converged programs requiring only a single CEGAR-CEGIS iteration. By avoiding the need to inline all function bodies at the top level, the approach substantially reduces the state space the underlying BMC engine must explore.

https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.27051

A Blueprint for Formal Verification of Apple corecrypto

Apple Security Research Team • Apple Security Research Blog • 22 May 2026

Apple open-sourced the corecrypto library alongside formal correctness proofs for its post-quantum ML-KEM and ML-DSA implementations, using a toolchain that combines Cryptol, SAW, and Isabelle with a custom Cryptol-to-Isabelle translator built by Galois and requiring over 50,000 proof steps across portable C and hand-optimised ARM64 assembly. The effort caught a real bug — a missing step in an early ML-DSA implementation that could silently corrupt cryptographic computations — that conventional testing would not have detected. The release provides a reproducible, publicly auditable formal-verification blueprint for production cryptographic code, a significant step for industry-scale formal methods.

https://security.apple.com/blog/formal-verification-corecrypto/
