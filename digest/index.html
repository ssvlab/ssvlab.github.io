ESBMC v8.3

ESBMC Development Team (esbmc.org / University of Manchester et al.) • 14 May 2026 • GitHub Release

Version 8.3 is the most feature-rich ESBMC release to date. On the C/C++ side it adds C++17 structured bindings, class template argument deduction (C++17/20), the spaceship operator (C++20), if consteval (C++23), explicit object parameters (C++23), and using enum (C++20), together with new operational models for <filesystem>, <any>, <variant>, <expected>, <span>, and related headers. The Python frontend gains comprehensive dataclass conformance, with/async with lowering, and new models for collections.deque, OrderedDict, random, and time. Cross-cutting improvements include CWE mapping with SARIF output, violation-witness validation, k-path coverage mode, a rewritten simplifier with reassociation, extended interval range analysis with rounding-mode support for IEEE division, and Bitwuzla as the new default SMT solver. Multiple automated nightly builds preceded the release between 4 and 25 May 2026.

Bounded Model Checking (BMC)

Finding Software Vulnerabilities in Large C Projects via Bounded Model Checking

Janislley Oliveira de Sousa, Bruno Carvalho de Farias, Thales Araujo da Silva, Eddie Batista de Lima Filho, Lucas C. Cordeiro • 2026 • International Journal on Software Tools for Technology Transfer (STTT), Springer

Bounded model checking is widely effective on small to medium codebases but struggles with the scale of real-world open-source C projects. This paper presents a practical methodology that combines automated source-code preprocessing, a guided verification workflow, and a function-level prioritisation mechanism to concentrate BMC effort on the most security-critical regions of large repositories. Evaluated on several popular open-source C projects, the approach discovered confirmed security vulnerabilities that prior analysis had missed, demonstrating that BMC can be productively applied well beyond benchmark-scale programmes. The work is authored by members of the ESBMC research group at the University of Manchester and collaborating institutions.

URL: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10009-026-00838-0

A Case Study in Firmware Verification: Applying Formal Methods to Intel® TDX Module

Dirk Beyer, Po-Chun Chien, Bo-Yuan Huang, Nian-Ze Lee, Thomas Lemberger • April 2026 (TACAS 2026, ETAPS Distinguished Paper) • Springer LNCS (TACAS 2026, Part I)

This ETAPS Distinguished Paper asks whether state-of-the-art C-programme analysers can be applied “out of the box” to security-critical production firmware. Six tools were evaluated—CBMC, ESBMC, KLEE, MOPSA, Ultimate Automizer, and CPAchecker—on the Intel Trust Domain Extensions (TDX) Module, a confidential-computing hypervisor component. 59% of verification tasks were solved by at least one tool; most failures were traced to firmware-specific coding idioms triggering front-end parsing errors rather than fundamental analysis limitations. HarnessForge was used to automate proof-harness generation, and all artefacts are publicly archived on Zenodo, making this one of the most openly reproducible firmware verification studies published.

Towards Formal Verification of Hybrid Synchronous Programs with Refinement Types

Serra Z. Dane, Jiawei Chen, Marc Pouzet, Jean-Baptiste Jeannin • 6 May 2026 (arXiv:2605.04377) • arXiv (cs.PL)

Synchronous languages extended with ordinary differential equations promise both rigorous modelling and executable controller code for cyber-physical systems. This paper introduces refinement types for such hybrid synchronous programmes, enabling static verification of real-valued temporal properties—stability margins, bounds on physical quantities— without requiring manual theorem-proving by the developer. The authors formalise a type system and evaluate it on autonomous-system controller examples, showing that refinement types can serve as a lightweight formal bridge between embedded C/C++ controller code and mathematical correctness guarantees, complementary to runtime model-checking approaches such as ESBMC.

Zero-Knowledge Model Checking

Pascal Berrang, Mirco Giacobbe, Jacob Swales, Xiao Yang (University of Birmingham) • May 2026 (arXiv:2605.00487) • arXiv (cs.CR / cs.LO)

This paper proposes a framework that allows a prover to demonstrate—via zero-knowledge cryptographic proofs—that a system satisfies a temporal specification without revealing the system itself. By combining deductive model-checking with zero-knowledge proof systems, the approach opens a path toward “confidential verification”: regulators, auditors, or customers could check safety and liveness properties of proprietary software without seeing its source code. A prototype is evaluated on small automata, establishing feasibility at modest scale and identifying open challenges for scaling to realistic programme sizes.

URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.00487

Daniel Kroening, Michael Tautschnig, and the CPROVER team (Oxford / Amazon) • 9 April 2026 • cprover.org (tool release)

EBMC 5.11 is the latest release of the Enhanced Bounded Model Checker for hardware designs, supporting Verilog 2005 and SystemVerilog 2017 as input languages with both bounded and unbounded model-checking engines, meaning it can both discover bugs and prove their absence. EBMC shares the CPROVER infrastructure with CBMC and is routinely compared with —and used alongside—software-level verification tools including ESBMC in hardware–software co-verification workflows. A Blueprint for Formal Verification of Apple corecrypto

Apple Security Research (Apple Inc.) • 22 May 2026 • Apple Security Research Blog

On 22 May 2026 Apple released the source code of its corecrypto cryptographic library alongside a detailed technical blog post explaining the custom formal verification pipeline used to certify its post-quantum ML-KEM and ML-DSA implementations. The workflow combines Isabelle/HOL, SAW (Software Analysis Workbench), Cryptol, and a new Cryptol-to-Isabelle translator developed with security firm Galois, generating over 50,000 individual proof steps to demonstrate conformance with FIPS 203 and FIPS 204 specifications. Formal verification caught at least one real bug —a missing step in an early ML-DSA implementation that would have silently corrupted cryptographic computations—that conventional testing would have missed. The entire toolchain is being shared with the broader research community; corecrypto runs on over 2.5 billion active Apple devices.

URL: https://security.apple.com/blog/formal-verification-corecrypto/
